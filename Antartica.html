Antarctica

Around 500 million years ago, a great supercontinent called Gondwana was formed. It was made up of several landmasses (a continent or large body of land), including what would become present-day India, Africa, South America, Madagascar, Australia and Antarctica. At this time, the land that would become Antarctica was sitting over the equator.

There are 17 species of penguin on the planet, but the eight you’ll most likely recognize live in Antarctica. Emperor Penguin, Adéile Penguin, Chinstrap Penguin, Macaroni Penguin, Gentoo Penguin, Rockhopper Penguin, Magellanic penguin, and lastly King penguin all reside in Antartica.

Gentoo Penguins are the fastest of all penguin species! These penguins can swim at speeds of up to 36km/h!

Most birds have hollow, air-filled bones to help them stay light for flight. Penguins adapted with solid bones instead. This helps them swim because solid bones reduce buoyancy—the tendency to float.

When did penguins arrive and where did they come from.

The earliest known penguins evolved shortly after the demise of the dinosaurs in the Cretaceous-Tertiary mass extinction. Roughly 66 million years ago species from the genus Waimanu lived in the waters off of New Zealand. Penguins are only found in the Southern Hemisphere. When penguin migrated to africa, around 12 million years ago when Drake's Passage between Antarctica and the southern tip of South America opened up fully and the Antarctic Circumpolar Current intensified which caused the glaciation of Antarctica and drove penguins that hadn't adapted to live in icy regions northward.

Contrary to many popular holiday cartoons, you’ll never see penguins and polar bears together in the wild. That’s because penguins live south of the equator while polar bears north of the equator in Arctic!

Do polar bears eat penguins?

Since there are no penguins in the Arctic and no polar bears on the Southern Hemisphere, penguins don’t get eaten by polar bears. This is only a common misconception. Polar bears and penguins have therefore never met. Read more about the penguins of Antarctica!

The Serengeti Plains are a grass savanna that has very dry but nutrient-rich volcanic sand. Around 2 million large plant-eating mammals live in the savanna.

Lake Vostok

Lake Vostok, also called Subglacial Lake Vostok or Lake East, largest lake in Antarctica. Located approximately 4km (2.5miles) beneath Russia's Vostok Station on the East Antarctic Ice Sheet.

Antarctica is not owned by any one nation. Antarctica is governed internationally through the Antarctic Treaty system. The Antarctic Treaty was signed in 1959 by 12 countries who had scientists in and around Antarctica at the time.
